Prevention Science

What the evidence actually shows.

Prevention science asks what changes behavior: which factors raise risk, which protect against it, which interventions work, and how risk moves over the course of a life. These are empirical questions with a substantial literature behind them, and the literature is uneven in ways worth being honest about.

This section organizes that literature. Where the evidence is strong, PRI says so. Where a widely repeated claim is not well supported, PRI says that too, and applies the confidence ratings set out in the Research Charter so a reader can tell the difference without taking anyone's word for it.

The Shape of the Evidence Base

One structural fact governs everything on this page. There is substantially more published research on managing people after an offense than on preventing a first one.cite That asymmetry is not a finding about what works. It is a finding about what has been funded.

It has a practical consequence: an intervention can look weakly supported simply because nobody has run the long, expensive study required to test it. PRI distinguishes between evidence of absence and absence of evidence, and labels which one it is looking at.

Primary Prevention Science

The science of stopping a first offense. The smallest literature and the one PRI is most concerned with building out.

Education programs

School and community programs, what they measurably change, and why knowledge and disclosure outcomes are easier to demonstrate than reductions in victimization.

Situational prevention

Research on modifying settings rather than predicting individuals: supervision structures, access, and opportunity in youth-serving organizations.

Help before harm

Outcomes reported by confidential services for adults seeking help before offending, and what the reporting environment does to uptake.

Bystander intervention

Adult responsibility research, and what shifts a person from noticing something to acting on it.

Protective Factors

What reduces risk, as distinct from what predicts it. Protective factor research is the most directly actionable part of the field, because most of these are things policy can change.

  • Stable housing, employment, and income
  • Family and social connection, and the measured effect of isolation
  • Treatment engagement and completion, distinct from treatment assignment
  • Supervision quality as distinct from supervision intensity
  • Offense-free time in the community

Risk, Measurement, and Its Limits

Actuarial risk assessment is the most methodologically developed part of this field and the most frequently misapplied. The instruments were built to estimate group-level rates. They are routinely used to make statements about individuals, which is a different claim requiring different evidence.

  • Static-99R and Static-2002R: construction, validation, and normative samples
  • Stable-2007 and dynamic risk factors
  • Desistance and offense-free time in the community
  • Aging and declining risk
  • Treatment effects on measured risk
  • The limits of group-based prediction applied to individual cases

How PRI Will Report It

Every topic in this section will carry a confidence rating, a statement of what the underlying studies did and did not measure, and a plain note where a finding is contested. Claims that serve a preferred conclusion get the same scrutiny as claims that do not.
