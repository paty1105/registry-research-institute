Comparative Prevention

What other countries do instead.

Other democracies face the same problem and have made materially different choices. The comparison is useful precisely because these are not hypothetical alternatives. They are operating systems in countries with comparable legal traditions, and several publish outcome data that the United States does not.

This section will document those systems as they actually work, not as advocates on either side describe them. Where a country reports good outcomes, PRI will report the caveats too: differences in population, base rates, reporting culture, and how offenses are defined all affect whether a comparison means anything.

What Each Country Profile Will Cover

Prevention investment

What the country spends on primary prevention, education, and public awareness, and how that compares to enforcement spending.

Treatment access

Whether treatment is funded, who qualifies, waiting times, and whether help is available to people who have not offended.

Supervision and review

How risk is assessed, how supervision is structured, and whether individualized review and exit pathways exist and are used.

Public notification

Whether information is public, restricted to agencies, or unavailable, and what evidence the country cites for its choice.

Reported outcomes

What is measured, at what interval, by whom, and whether the data is published in a form that permits independent checking.

Comparability

Definitional and cultural differences that limit what can fairly be concluded from any cross-national comparison.

Systems of Particular Interest

  • Germany. Preventive treatment offered to people who have not come to the attention of authorities, under confidentiality rules that make the offer credible.
  • United Kingdom. Statutory safeguarding duties for organizations working with children, and a management framework that is agency-facing rather than public.
  • Canada. Circles of support and accountability, and the reintegration research associated with them.
  • Netherlands and the Nordic countries. Treatment-oriented approaches with published outcome reporting.
  • Australia. Institutional safeguarding standards developed following a national inquiry into organizational responses.

Naming these systems is not an endorsement of any of them. Each will be documented with its reported outcomes, its criticisms, and the limits of what transfers to a different legal and cultural setting.
