Policy Impact

What current policy costs, and what it buys.

Public money spent on responding to sexual harm is a finite budget. This pillar asks three questions in order: what does the present approach cost, what measurable safety does it produce, and what else could those same resources buy? The first question is well documented. The second and third are where the evidence is thinnest, and where the Institute is concentrating.

The Opportunity Cost Question

Every dollar allocated to registry administration is a dollar not allocated to something else. Opportunity cost analysis asks what those same resources could produce if directed toward alternative public safety investments — particularly prevention-oriented programs with documented cost-effectiveness evidence.

This is not an argument against registration systems. It is a standard tool of public policy evaluation. Any responsible assessment of a public expenditure should examine what alternatives exist and what evidence supports each option.

Where This Sits in the Prevention Framework

Prevention research divides interventions into three levels by when they act: before any offense occurs, after risk is identified but before harm, and after an offense has happened. Almost all current public expenditure in this area sits at the third level, and most of that is administrative rather than therapeutic.

The framework, and what each level looks like in practice, is set out on the Prevention Framework page. Costs on this page are best read alongside it.

What the Research Shows on Prevention Costs

The Washington State Institute for Public Policy (WSIPP) — one of the most respected public policy research organizations in the United States — has conducted cost-benefit analyses comparing different approaches to managing sexual offense risk. Its research on the Special Sex Offender Sentencing Alternative (SSOSA), a community-based treatment option, found that a typical sex offender sentenced to incarceration costs state and local governments approximately five times as much as an offender who successfully completes community-based treatment with supervision.1

This does not mean treatment is always appropriate or that supervision costs nothing. WSIPP's framework explicitly accounts for treatment failures, revocation rates, and the full cost of community supervision. The finding is that when treatment succeeds, the cost differential is substantial — and that differential represents the opportunity cost of defaulting to incarceration and administrative tracking without treatment investment.

A 2023 systematic review published in peer-reviewed literature examined the economic evidence on community interventions to prevent child sexual abuse and exploitation, finding that prevention-oriented programs generally demonstrate positive cost-effectiveness ratios — but that the evidence base remains thin compared to the body of research on enforcement-oriented approaches.2 That asymmetry in research investment itself reflects a policy priority that has historically favored administrative systems over prevention infrastructure.

Administrative Infrastructure

Featured Source

Government Report — Primary Source

Sex Offender Registration and Monitoring: Triennial Program Review

Florida Office of Program Policy Analysis and Government Accountability (OPPAGA) — Report No. 24-09 — December 2024

Florida's OPPAGA conducted a statutory triennial review of the state's sex offender registration and monitoring program, examining administrative structure, registrant population, compliance, local ordinances, and removal processes. The report provides one of the most detailed publicly available assessments of a statewide registration system's operational scope and costs.

Key Data Points

86,207 Total registered sex offenders in Florida (statewide, including out-of-state)
30,907 In-state registrants (36% of total registry population)
196 Local ordinances imposing additional restrictions beyond state law
2,554 Registrants removed over three years — 92% due to death

Selected Findings

  • Multi-agency administration. Florida's registration system involves at least five state agencies: the Florida Department of Law Enforcement (FDLE), the Department of Corrections (FDC), the Department of Highway Safety and Motor Vehicles (DHSMV), the Department of Juvenile Justice (DJJ), and all 67 county sheriffs' offices.
  • Transient population growth. The percentage of registrants classified as transient (no fixed address) grew from 6% to 7% for sex offenders and from 9% to 12% for sexual predators over the review period — a trend the report links in part to the proliferation of residency restrictions making stable housing difficult to obtain.
  • Arrest-to-conviction gap widening. Arrests for registration violations increased 38% over ten years, while the conviction rate for those arrests fell from 60% to 45% — raising questions about charging practices, prosecutorial resources, and the proportion of technical violations being pursued.
  • Local ordinance proliferation. 196 local government ordinances now impose residency, presence, or other restrictions beyond state law. The report documents substantial geographic variation in restriction scope across Florida's counties and municipalities.
  • Registry removal is rare and largely driven by death. Of 2,554 removals over three years, the overwhelming majority — 92% — occurred because the registrant died. Removal through petition, completion of obligation, or other mechanisms accounted for a small fraction of exits.
  • Out-of-state registrants dominate the total count. Florida's registry of 86,207 includes a substantial population of individuals registered in other states. The in-state active population of 30,907 is the more operationally relevant figure for Florida agencies.

Source: Florida Office of Program Policy Analysis and Government Accountability (OPPAGA), Sex Offender Registration and Monitoring: Triennial Program Review, Report No. 24-09 (December 2024). Available through the Florida Action Foundation document library at floridaactionfoundation.org.

Why Administration and Cost Belong in a Prevention Conversation

Public policy debates about sex offender registration often focus on constitutional questions and behavioral science. The administrative and fiscal dimensions receive less attention — but they are essential to any complete evaluation.

Registration systems require sustained investment from law enforcement agencies, correctional departments, motor vehicle authorities, prosecutors, courts, and local governments. Understanding what that investment produces, and how it compares to alternative uses of those resources, is a prerequisite for evidence-based policy evaluation.

The OPPAGA report illustrates how a single state's registration system involves dozens of agencies, hundreds of local ordinances, and tens of thousands of registrants — while generating outcome data (arrest rates, conviction rates, removal rates) that raises important questions about administrative efficiency and the relationship between registration activity and public safety outcomes.
