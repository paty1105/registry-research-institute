Law and Public Policy

Law is where prevention policy becomes binding. This section examines both halves of that: the constitutional doctrines that determine what governments may do, and the design question of whether a given legal framework is built to produce measurable safety.

Those are separate tests. A statute can be constitutional and still fail to prevent harm. It can also be well designed and still be struck down. PRI treats the two questions independently and reports on each with its own evidence.

Policy Design Questions

  • What outcome is the statute intended to produce, and is that outcome measured?
  • Does the law distinguish between individuals by assessed risk, or apply uniformly by offense category?
  • Are there pathways for review, reassessment, and removal, and are they used?
  • Does the framework fund treatment and prevention, or only monitoring and enforcement?
  • What evidence was before the legislature at enactment, and has it been revisited since?
  • Are unintended effects on housing, employment, and family stability tracked?

Constitutional Issues

  • Ex Post Facto Clause
  • Substantive and procedural due process
  • Equal protection
  • First Amendment and internet identifier rules
  • Fourth Amendment and compelled reporting
