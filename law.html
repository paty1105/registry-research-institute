Law & Policy

Law and Public Policy

Law is where prevention policy becomes binding. This section examines both halves of that: the constitutional doctrines that determine what governments may do, and the design question of whether a given legal framework is built to produce measurable safety.

Those are separate tests. A statute can be constitutional and still fail to prevent harm. It can also be well designed and still be struck down. PRI treats the two questions independently and reports on each with its own evidence.

Policy Design Questions

  • What outcome is the statute intended to produce, and is that outcome measured?
  • Does the law distinguish between individuals by assessed risk, or apply uniformly by offense category?
  • Are there pathways for review, reassessment, and removal, and are they used?
  • Does the framework fund treatment and prevention, or only monitoring and enforcement?
  • What evidence was before the legislature at enactment, and has it been revisited since?
  • Are unintended effects on housing, employment, and family stability tracked?

Prevention Law

The legal questions that determine whether prevention is possible at all attract far less attention than the questions raised after an offense. They are the ones this section will build out first.

  • Mandatory reporting frameworks, and their effect on whether adults seek help before causing harm
  • Confidentiality and privilege for preventive treatment, and where the two conflict
  • Statutory safeguarding duties for schools, youth organizations, and faith communities
  • Prevention education mandates: what is required, what is funded, what is evaluated
  • Appropriations law and how prevention funding is authorized and sustained
  • Civil liability standards for institutions, and whether they drive real safeguarding change
  • Statutes of limitation and their effect on detection and deterrence

Constitutional Issues

Where governments act after an offense, constitutional limits apply. PRI reports these doctrines as legal analysis, distinct from the question of whether a policy prevents harm.

  • Ex Post Facto Clause
  • Substantive and procedural due process
  • Equal protection
  • First Amendment limits on compelled disclosure
  • Fourth Amendment and compelled reporting
  • Eighth Amendment proportionality arguments
  • Federalism and delegation in federal mandates
