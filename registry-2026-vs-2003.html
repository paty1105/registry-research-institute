The Registry of 2026 Is Not the Registry Considered in Smith v. Doe

A historical and evidence-based examination of the evolution of modern sex offender registration laws.

Published by: Registry Research Institute

Published: July 2026

Research status: Foundational overview — primary citations added

Evidence confidence: ● High for legislative history and Supreme Court record. Moderate for scientific claims pending full literature review in future publications.

This article will be updated as additional citations and state-specific documentation are completed.

Executive Summary

In 2003, the United States Supreme Court upheld Alaska's sex offender registration law in Smith v. Doe, concluding that the law functioned as a civil regulatory measure rather than criminal punishment.1 The Court's analysis was based on the specific statutory framework before it and the factual record available at that time.

More than two decades later, registry laws have evolved significantly. Across many jurisdictions, legislatures have expanded reporting obligations, increased administrative requirements, adopted new technologies, and introduced additional restrictions affecting housing, employment, travel, identification documents, and community participation.2

At the same time, scientific understanding of sexual offending has also advanced. Researchers have published decades of additional work on recidivism, desistance, individualized risk assessment, treatment outcomes, and behavioral change.3

To what extent do modern registry systems differ from the systems originally evaluated by the courts, and what implications might those changes have for public policy and constitutional analysis?

The purpose of this report is not to advocate for a predetermined conclusion. Rather, it is to organize historical developments, summarize current research questions, and provide readers with a transparent foundation for evaluating one of the most significant public safety policies in the United States.

Why This Matters

Sex offender registration laws influence housing, employment, education, immigration, travel, law enforcement practices, family life, and community notification. They also shape public perceptions of risk and public safety.

Because these policies affect multiple areas of society, they deserve periodic evaluation based on current evidence rather than assumptions. The Registry Research Institute was established to help answer questions that are often discussed but rarely examined in one place.

The Central Research Question

Rather than asking, "Should registries exist?" the Institute asks a narrower and more useful research question:

How should registry policies be evaluated using the best available legal, scientific, and empirical evidence?

That question recognizes that public policy should be examined in light of legislative history, constitutional developments, behavioral science, measurable outcomes, and ongoing research.

How Registry Laws Have Changed

The federal registration framework began with the Jacob Wetterling Crimes Against Children and Sexually Violent Offender Registration Act of 1994, which established a baseline requirement for states to maintain registries of persons convicted of certain offenses.4 Two years later, Megan's Law (1996) amended the Wetterling Act to require active community notification, directing states to release registry information to the public.5

In 2006, the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act created the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA), establishing a comprehensive federal framework that standardized tier classifications, expanded covered offenses, and imposed new obligations on states and territories.6

Since the 1990s, registry laws have expanded considerably in many jurisdictions. Depending on the jurisdiction, today's registration systems may include requirements relating to:

  • Periodic in-person reporting
  • Address verification
  • Employment reporting
  • Vehicle information
  • School enrollment
  • Temporary lodging
  • Internet identifiers
  • International travel notifications
  • Driver's license designations
  • Passport identifiers
  • Residency restrictions
  • Presence restrictions in certain locations
  • Expanded community notification

These requirements vary substantially by jurisdiction and continue to evolve through legislation and court decisions.7 One objective of the Institute is to document those differences through a searchable state-by-state database.

Scientific Knowledge Has Also Changed

Behavioral science did not stop evolving in 2003. Over the past two decades, researchers have published extensive work regarding sexual recidivism, desistance, dynamic risk factors, protective factors, aging and offending, treatment outcomes, individualized risk assessment, and actuarial instruments.3

A significant body of research has examined how actuarial instruments such as the Static-99R can be used to estimate relative risk at a group level, while also identifying the limitations of applying group-level statistics to individual cases.8 These developments have significantly expanded the scientific literature available to policymakers and courts. The Institute seeks to organize this body of research so readers can distinguish between well-supported findings, emerging evidence, and areas where important questions remain unanswered.

Administrative Violations and Public Understanding

One area requiring careful public explanation is the distinction between a new sexual offense allegation and a registration-rule violation. A person can be arrested for violating registry requirements without being accused of a new sexual offense. Examples may include failure to report an address change, employment change, vehicle information, travel plans, internet identifier, or temporary lodging.

These violations are legal offenses and should not be dismissed. But they should be categorized accurately. When public statistics combine or fail to clearly separate sexual recidivism, general recidivism, violent recidivism, and registration-compliance violations, public understanding can become distorted.9

RRI will evaluate how different jurisdictions define, prosecute, and report administrative registration violations. The goal is not to minimize legal obligations, but to clarify categories so policymakers and the public can interpret statistics accurately.

Evaluating Public Policy

Every public policy has intended objectives. Meaningful evaluation requires identifying measurable outcomes. Questions examined by the Registry Research Institute include:

  • How have registry systems changed over time?
  • Which policy objectives are clearly defined?
  • How are those objectives measured?
  • What evidence supports existing practices?
  • Where does evidence remain limited or mixed?
  • What additional research is needed?

These questions are intended to encourage careful examination rather than predetermined conclusions.

About This Series

This article is the first publication in the Registry Research Institute's ongoing series examining the historical development, scientific evidence, constitutional framework, and public policy implications of modern sex offender registration laws.

Future reports will examine the legislative evolution of registry systems, state-by-state statutory comparisons, constitutional litigation after Smith v. Doe, risk assessment and desistance research, administrative reporting requirements, community notification, residency restrictions, comparative international approaches, public policy evaluation, and the science of sexual recidivism.

Each publication will be supported by primary legal sources, peer-reviewed research, government reports, and transparent citations.

Looking Ahead

The Registry Research Institute does not ask readers to accept predetermined conclusions. Instead, we invite readers to examine the evidence, understand the evolution of registry policy, and participate in an informed discussion about one of the most consequential public safety systems in the United States.10

Evidence should inform policy. Understanding should precede opinion.
