Prevention Framework

What prevention actually means.

In public health, prevention is not one thing. It is three, distinguished by when they act: before anyone is harmed, after risk appears but before harm occurs, and after an offense has already happened. The distinction is not academic. It determines what a program is trying to do, who it reaches, and what counts as success.

Most public conversation about sexual harm operates almost entirely at the third level, and discusses it as though it were the whole field. That framing quietly rules out the question that matters most to a parent, a school board, or a youth program director: what stops the first offense from happening at all?

PrimaryBefore any offense

Stop harm before it starts

Interventions aimed at the whole population, not at identified individuals. The target is the incidence of first offenses.

  • Age-appropriate education on boundaries, consent, and body safety
  • Adult and bystander responsibility, shifting the burden of protection off children
  • Safeguarding policy in youth-serving organizations: supervision structures, screening, reporting routes
  • Confidential help for adults experiencing thoughts they are afraid of, before anyone is harmed
  • Strengthening family and community protective factors
SecondaryRisk identified, no offense

Interrupt the pathway

Interventions aimed at individuals or settings showing elevated risk, while there is still no offense to respond to.

  • Early identification and assessment
  • Mental health treatment and structured behavioral intervention
  • Support for young people displaying concerning behavior, handled developmentally
  • Situational intervention in settings where risk concentrates
TertiaryAfter an offense

Prevent it happening again

Interventions aimed at people who have already offended. The target is reoffending, and the mechanism should be documented behavioral change rather than tracking alone.

  • Evidence-based treatment and its completion rates
  • Supervision, and the difference between supervision quality and supervision intensity
  • Housing, employment, and family stability during reintegration
  • Monitoring, notification, and supervision systems

Where the investment actually sits

Public spending on sexual harm is concentrated almost entirely at the tertiary level, and within that level, weighted heavily toward administrative tracking rather than treatment.cite That is a description of how budgets are allocated, not an argument that any one component should be eliminated.

It does raise a question that any responsible policy evaluation has to ask. If the object is fewer victims, and the largest share of the budget acts only after there is already a victim, then the allocation deserves examination on its own terms. PRI documents that allocation on the Policy Impact page, using published state audit data.

Why the distinction matters in practice

Three concrete consequences follow from getting the levels straight.

It changes who the audience is

Tertiary prevention is a conversation between the state and people who have offended. Primary prevention is a conversation with parents, schools, coaches, faith communities, and youth organizations. Different audience, different message, different work.

It changes what counts as success

A tertiary program is measured by reoffense rates. A primary program is measured by the incidence of first offenses, which is harder to measure and takes longer to move. Applying the wrong metric makes good programs look ineffective.

It changes the evidence you need

The research base is not evenly distributed. There is substantially more published research on managing people after an offense than on preventing first offenses,cite and that asymmetry itself reflects where funding has gone.

Start here for the community-facing version: Preventing a First Offense sets out what primary prevention looks like in practice, written for parents, educators, and organizations rather than for specialists.

What PRI will and will not claim here

This framework is descriptive and widely used across public health. Applying it to sexual harm is not a novel position and is not, in itself, an argument for or against any specific policy.

Where PRI does take a position, it is a narrow one: outcomes should be measured, allocations should be justified by evidence, and a claim that a policy prevents harm should be supported by data showing that it does. The Research Charter sets out the confidence ratings used to distinguish well-supported findings from contested and unsupported ones.
