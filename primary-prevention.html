Primary Prevention

Preventing a first offense.

Every system that responds after harm has already happened is, by definition, too late for that victim. Primary prevention is the work of reducing how often there is a first victim at all. It is the least funded part of the field and the part most people assume does not exist.

If you are worried about a child right now, or you need to report something, contact local law enforcement or child protective services. The National Sexual Assault Hotline is available 24 hours a day at 800-656-4673.verify This page is research and education, not a crisis service.

Prevention effort has to be aimed where harm actually occurs

Most public prevention messaging is built around a picture of the stranger: an unknown adult, an unfamiliar place, a child who needs to be taught to run. The research picture is different. Sexual harm against children is overwhelmingly committed by someone the child already knows and trusts, inside relationships and institutions the family has accepted.cite

The second finding matters just as much for policy. A large share of people who commit sexual offenses have no prior conviction for one, which means that at the moment they cause harm they appear on no registry and no watch list.cite A system built entirely around monitoring people already convicted cannot, by construction, reach them.

Neither point is an argument that post-conviction systems do nothing. Both are arguments that a prevention strategy consisting only of those systems has a large and predictable gap in it, and that the gap is where most first offenses occur.

What primary prevention looks like in practice

1. Put responsibility on adults, not on children

Programs that teach children to recognize and refuse unsafe touch have value, and children should have that vocabulary. But a strategy that rests on a child successfully detecting and resisting a trusted adult has placed the burden in the wrong place. The adults around a child are the ones with the capacity to notice, interrupt, and report, and prevention programming that is aimed at them addresses the actual point of leverage.

2. Build safeguarding into organizations, not just training days

Youth-serving organizations are where structural prevention is most tractable, because the conditions that allow harm are largely design choices. Situational prevention asks what the environment permits, rather than trying to predict which individual is dangerous.

  • Reduce or eliminate unobserved one-to-one access between adults and children
  • Make sightlines, room use, and transport arrangements explicit policy
  • Set rules for private digital contact between staff and young people
  • Provide a reporting route that does not depend on the reporter's judgment about whether something is serious enough
  • Respond to boundary-testing behavior early, when it is still a policy matter
  • Screen and reference-check, while treating screening as a floor and not a strategy

Screening deserves particular care. Background checks filter people with records. Since most first offenses are committed by people without one, checks are necessary but cannot be the substance of a safeguarding plan.

3. Teach consent and boundaries as ordinary developmental education

Age-appropriate education on bodies, boundaries, privacy, and consent gives young people language for what is happening to them and makes disclosure more likely. It also establishes norms for their own future conduct. Evaluations of school-based programs generally report improved knowledge and disclosure; measuring effects on actual victimization rates is considerably harder.cite PRI reports both parts of that finding rather than the first alone.

4. Make help available before anyone is harmed

Some adults experience sexual thoughts about children that they do not want and have not acted on. Whether that person can find confidential, competent help is a prevention question with a direct bearing on whether a child is harmed. In most of the United States, seeking that help carries a real risk of reporting consequences, and the practical effect is that people do not seek it.

Two established services are relevant here. Stop It Now operates a confidential helpline in the United States for adults concerned about their own thoughts or someone else's behavior.verify The Prevention Project Dunkelfeld, based at Charité in Berlin, offers treatment to people who have not come to the attention of authorities, under German confidentiality rules that make the offer credible.cite

PRI takes no position here on how mandatory reporting law should be written. It notes only that the reporting framework and the availability of preventive treatment are connected, and that the connection is rarely examined when either is debated.

5. Strengthen the conditions that protect families

Prevention research consistently finds that harm concentrates where supervision is thin, isolation is high, and adults are under sustained stress. Interventions that address those conditions are not usually labeled sexual harm prevention, and are rarely counted as such in budgets, but they act on documented risk factors.cite

What the evidence does and does not support

Honesty about the limits is part of the argument, not a concession against it. Applying the confidence ratings from the Research Charter:

Moderate confidence: school-based education programs improve knowledge and increase disclosure.cite

Limited evidence: direct measured effect of most primary prevention programs on victimization incidence, largely because the studies required are long, expensive, and rarely funded.cite

Moderate confidence: organizational and situational measures reduce opportunity in institutional settings.cite

Evidence gap: comparative effectiveness of primary prevention against tertiary monitoring, per dollar spent. The comparison is asserted in both directions and rigorously established in neither.

That last line is the honest state of the field, and it is a reason to fund the research rather than a reason to defer the question. See Policy Impact for the cost side of the same comparison.

If you are here for your organization

A reasonable starting sequence: write down where unobserved one-to-one contact can currently occur, close what can be closed and supervise what cannot, publish a reporting route that any member of staff or any parent can use without needing permission, and decide in advance who responds when a report arrives. Most institutional failures documented after the fact were failures at one of those four points rather than failures of screening.cite

Editorial: sourcing required before publication

  1. Proportion of child sexual abuse committed by known and trusted individuals. Use a primary source, not a secondary advocacy citation.
  2. Proportion of those committing sexual offenses with no prior sexual offense conviction. Source carefully, this figure varies by jurisdiction, offense definition, and study design, and is frequently misquoted.
  3. School-based prevention program evaluations. A systematic review or meta-analysis is preferable to individual trials.
  4. Prevention Project Dunkelfeld. Cite the Charité program literature directly, and characterize reported outcomes conservatively.
  5. Stop It Now helpline details and the National Sexual Assault Hotline number. Verify both against the operators' own current pages before publication.
  6. Risk and protective factor findings on supervision, isolation, and family stress.
  7. Institutional failure analysis supporting the closing paragraph.
  8. Remove every cite and verify marker and this block once sources are attached.
