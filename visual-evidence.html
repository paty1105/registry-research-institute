Visual Evidence Lab

Turning legal complexity into verifiable public evidence.

Citation-ready graphics for attorneys, journalists, legislators, researchers, educators, and families.

Prevention arguments fail when the underlying numbers are unclear. These modules do the clarifying work: separating categories that are routinely conflated, showing how risk changes over time, tracing how policy expanded and what each change was meant to achieve, and documenting where prevention resources actually go. Each module is built to be cited and will link to a statute, court opinion, or government source.

Graphic 03

Violations vs. New Offenses

This module explains how a registrant can be re-arrested for violating registry rules without being accused of a new sexual offense. The goal is not to minimize legal obligations, but to clarify categories that can be misunderstood by the public.

Registry-Related Arrests

New Criminal Conduct

New allegation involving harm, victimization, or other criminal conduct.

Registration-Rule Violation

Failure to comply with reporting obligations such as address, vehicle, internet identifier, employment, travel, or temporary lodging rules.

Why this matters

Public statistics can be frightening when categories are not clearly separated.

PRI standard

Every statistic should identify whether it concerns sexual recidivism, general recidivism, violent recidivism, or registration compliance violations.

Research question

How often do lawmakers, agencies, or media reports conflate these categories?

Graphic 06

Where Prevention Money Goes

Planned module. Public spending on sexual harm is concentrated at the tertiary level, after an offense has already occurred. This graphic will show the distribution across all three prevention levels, using published appropriations and audit data.

Primary

Education, safeguarding, adult responsibility, and confidential help before harm. Acts on the whole population; measured by the incidence of first offenses.

Secondary

Early identification, treatment access, and situational intervention where risk is elevated but no offense has occurred.

Tertiary

Treatment, supervision, reintegration, registration, and monitoring after an offense. Where nearly all current expenditure sits.

Research note: this module will not publish until appropriations figures for each level are sourced to state or federal budget documents. See the Prevention Framework for the underlying model and Policy Impact for the cost evidence gathered so far.
