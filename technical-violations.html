Measuring prevention honestly.

A forthcoming analysis of why prevention arguments collapse when the underlying statistics mix categories, and what a defensible measure of public safety would look like.

This page is a placeholder. An earlier version of this article was written around a different editorial frame and has been withdrawn pending rewrite. The URL is preserved so existing links continue to resolve.

What the replacement will cover

  • Why a single rearrest figure cannot measure whether harm was prevented
  • The categories that must be reported separately for any prevention claim to be testable
  • How conflated statistics travel through agency reporting, media coverage, and legislative debate
  • What outcome measures prevention programs should be held to, and who currently collects them
  • A proposed reporting standard for jurisdictions that want their claims to be verifiable
