Research Brief

Administrative Registry Violations vs. New Sexual Offenses

A plain-language research brief explaining why rearrest and violation statistics must distinguish compliance offenses from allegations of new sexual victimization.

Research Status: Foundational public education brief

Evidence Confidence: Moderate for the conceptual distinction; source-specific review required for jurisdiction-level statistics

PRI Standard: This page avoids claiming that all official statistics are misleading. Instead, it explains why each dataset must be examined for how it defines and separates offense categories.

The Core Issue

Public discussions about sex offender registries often use terms such as rearrest, recidivism, failure to comply, and new offense as though they mean the same thing. They do not.

A person on a registry may be arrested for a new sexual offense, a nonsexual offense, a supervision violation, or an administrative registration violation. These categories have very different meanings for public safety, but they can appear similar in public conversation when they are not carefully separated.

Before statistics are used to justify expanding registry laws, the public should know what the statistic actually counts.

What Is an Administrative Registry Violation?

An administrative registry violation generally involves failure to follow a registration rule. Depending on state law, examples may include failing to report a change of address, employment, vehicle information, temporary lodging, school enrollment, internet identifiers, or travel plans.

These violations can be criminal offenses. However, they are not the same thing as an allegation that a person committed a new sexual assault or abuse offense.

Registrant Rearrest or Violation Event

New Criminal Conduct

May include a new sexual offense, new violent offense, or other alleged criminal act.

Administrative Registry Violation

May involve reporting, address, vehicle, employment, travel, internet identifier, or other compliance requirements.

Why This Distinction Matters

When administrative violations are discussed without context, members of the public may reasonably assume that a violation means a new act of sexual victimization occurred. In some cases, that may be true. In other cases, the violation may involve paperwork, timing, address verification, travel reporting, or another compliance obligation.

Public safety policy depends on accurate categories. If lawmakers, journalists, or agencies cite rearrest numbers without distinguishing what kind of conduct was involved, the public may receive an incomplete picture of what the data actually shows.

What PRI Will Track

  • Whether reports separate sexual recidivism from general recidivism
  • Whether reports separate new criminal charges from registration violations
  • Whether technical violations are counted as recidivism
  • Whether legislative testimony defines the statistics being cited
  • Whether public statements distinguish compliance violations from new victimization
  • Whether state agencies publish raw category-level data

Registry Policy and First-Time Offending

One major limitation of any registry system is structural: registries identify people already convicted of qualifying offenses. A registry cannot list someone who has never been convicted of a qualifying offense. For that reason, registry policy should be evaluated alongside broader prevention strategies aimed at first-time offending, early intervention, reporting, victim services, and community education.

The federal SMART Office summarizes adult sex-offender recidivism research and notes that studies vary by definitions, follow-up periods, and outcome measures. That variation is exactly why PRI separates categories before drawing policy conclusions.1

What the Research Often Shows

Research on sexual recidivism frequently finds that sexual reoffense rates are lower than commonly assumed, while general rearrest or technical violation rates may be higher depending on how the study defines its outcome. A widely cited Canadian study by Harris and Hanson concluded that most sexual offenders do not sexually reoffend and that age and prior sexual convictions matter in assessing risk.2

This does not mean risk is zero. It means categories matter. A study measuring any rearrest is not measuring the same outcome as a study measuring new sexual conviction. A policy debate that collapses those categories can mislead the public even when the underlying data is technically accurate.

Research Questions for the Institute

  • How do state agencies define and report registry violations?
  • How often are registry violations described publicly as evidence of sexual dangerousness?
  • What percentage of registrant rearrests involve administrative noncompliance rather than new sexual offenses?
  • Which states publish enough data to distinguish these categories?
  • How have technical violations influenced legislative expansions?

What Responsible Reporting Should Say

When citing statistics about registrants, responsible reporting should identify the outcome being measured. For example:

Less Clear

“Registrants reoffended at a high rate.”

More Clear

“The report measured rearrest for any offense, including administrative registration violations; it did not separately report new sexual convictions.”

PRI Position

PRI does not claim that administrative violations are irrelevant. Compliance rules may serve legitimate administrative purposes. The point is narrower and more important: administrative violations should not be confused with new sexual victimization when the public is evaluating registry effectiveness.

Accurate public safety analysis requires accurate categories.

Sources
