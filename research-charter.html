Institutional Charter

The PRI Research Charter

The Prevention Research Institute exists to identify, evaluate, and communicate strategies that prevent sexual harm and produce measurable, lasting public safety.

Mission

To become a trusted evidence-based resource on primary prevention, effective treatment, risk reduction, desistance, reintegration, victim-centered safety, and responsible public policy.

Core Question

What does the evidence show will prevent harm most effectively?

Research Principles

  • We begin with questions, not conclusions.
  • Every factual claim should be traceable to a source.
  • Research, analysis, commentary, and opinion should be clearly distinguished.
  • Conflicting evidence should be represented fairly.
  • Corrections should be published openly.
  • Public safety should be measured by outcomes, not assumptions.
  • Good policy should be continuously evaluated as evidence evolves.

Five Pillars

Prevention

Primary, secondary, and tertiary prevention; education and safeguarding; protective factors; help-seeking before harm; and the allocation of resources across the three levels.

Law

Constitutional doctrine, court decisions, statutes, legislative history, and evolving legal standards.

Science

Behavioral science, criminology, risk assessment, desistance, treatment outcomes, and recidivism research.

Policy

Prevention investment, treatment access, supervision, reintegration, public education, legal frameworks, costs, and measurable outcomes.

Data

Research summaries, court databases, state profiles, visual evidence, timelines, and public-source documentation.

Evidence Confidence Ratings

PRI will classify research topics according to the strength of available evidence.

High Confidence: Multiple systematic reviews, meta-analyses, or consistent findings across high-quality studies.

Moderate Confidence: Good evidence exists, but there are meaningful limitations or conflicting results.

Limited Evidence: Early research or findings that are not yet well established.

Evidence Gap: A commonly asserted claim lacks sufficient empirical support or needs additional study.

Questions Worth Examining

  • Which interventions prevent a first offense, and what evidence supports them?
  • What does effective prevention education look like, and what does it measurably change?
  • Can adults seeking help before causing harm find it, and what happens when they do?
  • Which organizational safeguarding practices reduce opportunity in youth-serving settings?
  • How is public spending distributed across the three levels of prevention?
  • Which interventions reduce reoffending?
  • How have reporting obligations changed since 1994?
  • What does actuarial science tell us about long-term risk?
  • How are technical violations reported across jurisdictions?
  • What are the measurable impacts of residency restrictions?
  • How do international prevention and supervision systems differ?
  • How do housing, employment, treatment access, and family stability affect risk?
  • What evidence exists regarding deterrence?
  • How should policymakers distinguish between new sexual offenses and administrative reporting violations?
  • What outcomes should be used to evaluate prevention policy?
Editorial Standard: PRI is not built to persuade by selective evidence. It is built to organize the evidence comprehensively and transparently.
