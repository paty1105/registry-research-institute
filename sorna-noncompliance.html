SORNA Non-Compliance: Which States Chose a Different Path and Why

Eighteen years after Congress enacted the Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act, only 18 of 50 states have substantially implemented its requirements. This article documents the compliance landscape, the documented reasons states have not complied, and what this divergence reveals about the relationship between federal registration mandates and state-level public safety priorities.

Published by: Registry Research Institute

Published: July 2026

Research status: Foundational overview — primary citations from DOJ SMART Office records

Evidence confidence: ● High for compliance status data (DOJ SMART Office, September 2024). State-specific rationale drawn from publicly documented legislative records, auditor reports, and legislative testimony where available.

Executive Summary

The Sex Offender Registration and Notification Act (SORNA), enacted as Title I of the Adam Walsh Child Protection and Safety Act of 2006, established a federal minimum standard for sex offender registration and notification systems across the United States.1 States that substantially implement SORNA's requirements avoid a 10% reduction in their Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant (JAG) funding. States that do not comply have that funding withheld and reallocated.2

As of September 2024, the DOJ SMART Office reports that only 18 states, 4 territories, and 137 federally recognized tribes have substantially implemented SORNA — meaning 32 states have chosen to forfeit federal funding rather than fully comply with the national framework.3

This is not a minor administrative gap. It represents a majority of U.S. states making a deliberate policy choice. Understanding why requires examining the documented reasons states have given — which range from constitutional concerns and cost to explicit policy disagreements about what registration systems should accomplish.

Why States Have Not Complied

Non-compliance is not uniform in its causes. The Government Accountability Office documented several categories of challenges in a 2013 report, and subsequent state-level records add further texture.4 The documented reasons fall into four broad categories:

1. Implementation Cost

The GAO found that cost was among the most frequently cited barriers to SORNA compliance. States reported that the legislative, regulatory, and technological changes required to meet SORNA's minimum standards would exceed the value of the Byrne JAG funding they would lose by not complying. In some states, the cost of compliance was estimated to be multiples of the funding penalty — making non-compliance the fiscally rational choice.4

2. Inconsistency with State Law

Several states had existing registration frameworks that differed from SORNA's requirements in ways that would require significant legislative action to resolve. Some states had individualized risk-based systems, different tier structures, or different offense coverage rules that their legislatures were unwilling to override in favor of SORNA's categorical approach.

3. Constitutional Concerns

Some states and their attorneys general raised concerns about SORNA's retroactive application — requiring individuals to register under new, more burdensome rules for offenses committed before SORNA's enactment. Courts in several states found that retroactive application of expanded registration requirements violated state constitutional provisions, creating conflicts between SORNA compliance and binding state court decisions.5

4. Policy Disagreement — Effectiveness and Prevention

A smaller but notable category involves states that have explicitly questioned whether SORNA's framework produces the public safety outcomes it is designed to achieve. Some state legislators and policy officials have argued that the resources required for SORNA compliance would be more effectively deployed in treatment, supervision, and prevention programs with documented evidence of reducing sexual offending — rather than in expanded administrative tracking systems whose effectiveness is disputed in the research literature.6

The Natural Experiment

The divergence between compliant and non-compliant states creates a natural research opportunity. If SORNA compliance produces measurable public safety benefits — lower rates of sexual reoffense, improved victim outcomes, better law enforcement coordination — those benefits should be visible in outcome comparisons between compliant and non-compliant states over the eighteen years since enactment.

To date, rigorous comparative outcome research between SORNA-compliant and non-compliant states is limited. The question of whether compliance status predicts any measurable public safety outcome — controlling for population, offense rates, and other variables — remains an open research question that the Registry Research Institute intends to pursue as state-level data becomes available.

Research Question: Do states that have substantially implemented SORNA demonstrate measurably different sexual recidivism rates, clearance rates, or victim reporting rates compared to non-compliant states, controlling for baseline differences? This question cannot yet be answered definitively from available data. RRI will document evidence as it emerges.

Funding Mechanics

States that do not substantially implement SORNA lose 10% of their Edward Byrne Memorial Justice Assistance Grant allocation.2 That withheld funding is reallocated — either to compliant states or back to the non-compliant state if it applies for reallocation funds to be used solely for SORNA implementation activities.

As of 2024, 22 non-compliant states have been approved to apply for reallocation funding to be used toward SORNA implementation — meaning even the mechanism designed to incentivize compliance primarily funds more compliance activity rather than alternative public safety investments.3

This funding structure means that the federal incentive system channels resources toward administrative standardization regardless of whether that standardization produces better outcomes than the approaches non-compliant states have chosen.

What This Means for Research

The majority non-compliance with SORNA after eighteen years is, itself, evidence of something. Whether it reflects bureaucratic inertia, principled policy disagreement, fiscal calculation, or some combination of all three varies by state and requires state-specific documentation. The Registry Research Institute will develop individual state profiles examining compliance status, stated rationale, and available outcome data as part of its ongoing State Law Database project.

The most important research question the compliance gap raises is the one least studied: does the federal registration framework, as implemented by SORNA, produce public safety outcomes that justify its administrative cost — in both compliant and non-compliant states — relative to what the same resources could produce if directed elsewhere?
